I Am Because We Are — The Power of Connection

May 21, 2026

Exploring Ubuntu philosophy and how collective belonging shapes our sense of purpose and fulfillment.

There is a Nguni Bantu philosophy that says: Umuntu ngumuntu ngabantu. A person is a person through other persons. This is Ubuntu — a worldview that places human interdependence at the heart of everything. Not as a nice idea, but as a lived truth that shapes how we move, how we lead, and how we heal.

In a world that celebrates radical individualism, Ubuntu offers a counter-narrative. It does not say that the individual does not matter. It says the individual only fully becomes themselves in the presence of others. Our gifts are not truly ours until they are given away. Our healing is not complete until it ripples outward. Our purpose is never just personal — it is communal.

I have seen this in my own coaching work. The women who come to me often arrive with a quiet ache of disconnection. They have achieved things — degrees, careers, titles — but they feel alone in the journey. What they are longing for is not simply more success. They are longing to be known, to belong, and to contribute to something beyond themselves. That longing is Ubuntu calling.

Practicing Ubuntu in daily life looks like listening deeply before speaking. It looks like celebrating others' wins without diminishing your own. It looks like showing up for your community not when it is convenient, but when it is needed. It looks like recognising that when one of us rises, we all rise.

As you explore your own purpose, ask yourself: who shaped me? Whose shoulders am I standing on? How does my Ikigai serve the collective? When you root your individual purpose in communal belonging, you discover that you were never meant to do this alone — and that is not a weakness. It is the source of your greatest strength.
